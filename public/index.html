Final Visualization

My current research is focusing on the best way to understand near real-time (NRT) monitoring of deforestation such that we can more effectively mitigate illegal logging. At the end of these 4 years, I plan to have an app that forest managers can use showing probability of deforestation across a landscape. The case will be something like this: there is a map made up of 10m pixels that are all blue, indicating low probability of deforestation. Suddenly, the next day one pixel is dark red, while the pixels around it extending to a 25km diameter circle are brightening shades toward blue. This indicates that last night, someone cut down trees in the dark red pixel, and now because of that, the surrounding pixels have an elevated risk of deforestation compared to before.

As I currently don't have the data or code for this yet, I decided to create a proxy:

Methods

The data shown here is NDVI for late December 2018 - January 2020 as obtained from cloud-masked Landsat 8 Collection 1 Surface Reflectance Tier 1 via Google Earth Engine (see this link for example code, and scroll down on the right panel to see some charts). Each circle's size relates to the size of the disturbance itself, with larger disturbances (more deforestation) having the larger circles. I should note the size range of 5-25km is completely arbitrary given my lack of full understanding at this point. I decided to double-encode the meaning of the circles as well, by showing both size and color differences. Originally I was going to use a single red color, but I quickly realized it was near impossible to accurately distinguish the individual sizes. I was also going to have both circles and markers to show the points, but I decided against it after seeing how the markers obscured the circles themselves. With some of the points already very small, this was not ideal.

Challenges

Originally I was going to make this visualization using Shiny in R, but I was having problems with hosting via shiny-apps.io, so I abandoned it and moved to htmlwidgets instead. The final product here relies heavily on the "leaftime" package, for whose existence I'm grateful. Some challenges were present along the way, however. For example, as long as it took me to figure out the Google Earth Engine code, it took me nearly as long (or what felt nearly as long) to tweak Shiny and htmlwidgets to work within R. One aspect I was keen on adding was having small lightning bolt markers pop up when there was a confirmed disturbance in the data, but unfortunately I was unable to get this to work.

About Me


Hi! I'm Ian, a new PhD student studying how to save forests, from the US to Myanmar to Peru.


Prior to looking at forests from space, I was inspecting them from their roots. For my last position I worked with the Smithsonian understanding forest resiliency near Shenandoah National Park, as part of the ForestGEO network. Before that, I researched fire in the savannah-rainforest mosaic of central Gabon.
